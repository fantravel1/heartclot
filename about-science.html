About the Science

Understanding the evidence, research methodology, and scientific approach behind our recommendations

Evidence-Based Medicine Meets Clinical Experience

This website combines peer-reviewed scientific research with 35+ years of clinical cardiology experience. We believe in presenting information that is:

  • Evidence-based: Supported by published research when available
  • Clinically validated: Tested in real-world patient care over decades
  • Mechanistically sound: Based on understood biological mechanisms
  • Transparently presented: With citations, limitations, and alternative perspectives acknowledged

Important Context

Medical science is constantly evolving. What we present here reflects current understanding based on available evidence and clinical observation. Some recommendations are supported by extensive randomized controlled trials, while others are based on mechanistic studies and clinical experience. We strive to make these distinctions clear.

Understanding Levels of Evidence

Not all medical evidence is created equal. Here's how different types of studies rank in terms of reliability:

Level Type of Evidence Reliability Example
1A Systematic review of RCTs Highest Meta-analysis of multiple fasting trials
1B Individual RCT with narrow CI Very High Controlled trial of time-restricted eating
2A Systematic review of cohort studies High Review of insulin resistance outcomes
2B Individual cohort study Moderate Framingham Heart Study data
3 Case-control study Moderate Comparing heart attack patients to controls
4 Case series Low-Moderate Surgeon's experience with 30,000 patients
5 Expert opinion Low Clinical judgment based on experience

RCT = Randomized Controlled Trial; CI = Confidence Interval

Important Note: While RCTs are the "gold standard," they're not always available or even ethical for every question. For example, we can't randomly assign people to smoke for 20 years to prove causation. Sometimes we must rely on mechanistic studies, cohort data, and clinical experience.

How to Interpret Medical Studies

Key Questions to Ask

1. Who funded the study?

Industry-funded research can have bias. Independent or NIH-funded studies are generally more reliable. All studies on this site are from peer-reviewed journals.

2. What was the study design?

RCTs > Cohort studies > Case-control > Case series > Expert opinion

3. How many people were studied?

Larger sample sizes generally provide more reliable results. Studies with n<50 should be viewed cautiously.

4. How long was the study?

For chronic disease outcomes (like heart disease), longer studies are more meaningful. A 2-week study tells us less than a 10-year study.

5. What outcomes were measured?

Hard outcomes (death, heart attack) > Surrogate outcomes (cholesterol levels, blood pressure). Not all surrogate markers translate to actual health improvements.

Common Pitfalls

  • Correlation ≠ Causation: Just because two things occur together doesn't mean one causes the other
  • Relative vs Absolute Risk: "50% reduction" sounds impressive, but if baseline risk is 0.2%, reducing to 0.1% is only 0.1% absolute benefit
  • Statistical Significance ≠ Clinical Significance: A statistically significant finding may not be clinically meaningful
  • Publication Bias: Negative studies are less likely to be published, skewing our view of the evidence

Study Limitations & Research Gaps

It's important to acknowledge what we don't know and where research is limited:

Areas with Strong Evidence

  • Insulin resistance precedes type 2 diabetes by years
  • Visceral fat is metabolically harmful
  • Coronary calcium score predicts cardiovascular events
  • Time-restricted eating improves metabolic markers
  • Reducing refined carbohydrates improves insulin sensitivity

Areas with Emerging Evidence

  • Optimal fasting insulin targets (studies use different cutoffs)
  • Long-term safety of extended fasting protocols (>5 years)
  • Individual variation in fasting responses
  • Optimal macronutrient ratios for different populations

Areas Needing More Research

  • Head-to-head comparisons of different fasting protocols
  • Long-term cardiovascular outcomes (most studies are 1-2 years)
  • Optimal testing frequency for early detection
  • Personalized approaches based on genetics or microbiome

The Role of Clinical Experience

Dr. Pradip Jamnadas has performed over 30,000 cardiac procedures in 35+ years of practice. This clinical experience provides:

Valuable Insights

  • Pattern Recognition: Seeing thousands of patients reveals patterns not always captured in studies
  • Real-World Effectiveness: What actually works in diverse patient populations
  • Early Warning Signals: Identifying problems before they become obvious
  • Individual Variation: Understanding that protocols need personalization

Limitations of Anecdotal Evidence

  • Confirmation Bias: We tend to remember successes more than failures
  • No Control Group: Can't know what would have happened without intervention
  • Selection Bias: Patients who seek certain doctors may differ from general population
  • Confounding Variables: Multiple factors change simultaneously

Best Practice

We combine clinical experience with published research. When recommendations are based primarily on clinical observation rather than RCTs, we state this explicitly.

Expert Consensus & Disagreement

Medicine is not monolithic. Experts sometimes disagree, and that's healthy for scientific progress.

Areas of Broad Consensus

  • Metabolic syndrome increases cardiovascular risk
  • Visceral adiposity is harmful independent of total body fat
  • Insulin resistance is a key driver of metabolic disease
  • Lifestyle interventions can reverse early-stage metabolic dysfunction
  • Standard diabetes screening misses years of pre-diabetic damage

Areas of Active Debate

  • Optimal fasting approach: Daily time-restriction vs periodic longer fasts
  • Dietary approach: Low-carb vs Mediterranean vs plant-based
  • Cholesterol targets: How aggressively to lower LDL
  • Testing thresholds: What constitutes "optimal" vs "normal"

We present one evidence-based perspective while acknowledging that other qualified experts may have different approaches. Your healthcare provider may recommend differently, and that's okay.

Our Scientific Approach

How We Evaluate Evidence

  1. Mechanistic Plausibility: Does it make biological sense?
  2. Animal Studies: What do preclinical models show?
  3. Human Observational Data: What do cohort studies reveal?
  4. Randomized Trials: What do controlled experiments demonstrate?
  5. Clinical Outcomes: What actually happens to patients?
  6. Safety Profile: What are the potential harms?

Principles We Follow

  • Transparency: We cite sources so you can verify claims
  • Humility: We acknowledge uncertainty and limitations
  • Evolution: We update information as new evidence emerges
  • Nuance: We avoid oversimplification while remaining accessible
  • Patient-Centered: We focus on outcomes that matter to patients

How to Verify Information

We encourage you to fact-check everything. Here's how:

1. Check Our References

Every major claim on this site is linked to peer-reviewed research. Click the citation numbers to view the original studies on PubMed.

2. Use PubMed

Search for topics yourself at pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov

Example search: "insulin resistance cardiovascular disease"

3. Review Systematic Reviews

Look for Cochrane reviews and meta-analyses which synthesize multiple studies.

4. Consult Multiple Sources

  • UpToDate: Evidence-based clinical decision support
  • DynaMed: Point-of-care clinical reference
  • Medical textbooks: Established knowledge
  • Professional guidelines: AHA, ACC, ADA recommendations

5. Talk to Your Doctor

Bring this information to your healthcare provider. A good doctor will engage with the evidence and help you determine what's right for your situation.

Red Flags for Misinformation
  • Claims that sound "too good to be true"
  • No citations or references provided
  • Selling products or services
  • Attacking all mainstream medicine
  • Claiming "doctors don't want you to know"
  • Promising "cures" instead of "improvements"

We avoid these pitfalls by: Providing citations, having no commercial interests, respecting evidence-based medicine while questioning outdated paradigms, and making realistic claims about outcomes.

Our Commitment

We are committed to presenting accurate, evidence-based information while being transparent about limitations and uncertainties. Medical knowledge evolves, and we will update our content as new high-quality evidence emerges.
